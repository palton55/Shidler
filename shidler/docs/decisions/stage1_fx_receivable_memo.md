## Executive Summary (≤150 words)
We expect to receive €5,000,000 in 90 days from a European customer while our reporting currency is USD. The USD value of that inflow depends on EUR/USD at conversion. This exposure matters because euro weakness would reduce the USD proceeds, lowering revenue and potentially compressing margins and earnings guidance. Hedging is worth considering because it trades some upside for reduced volatility and more reliable planning.

---

## Background & Objectives
Current FX markets remain volatile and forecasting exchange rates is difficult. The problem is simple: if EUR depreciates, the USD value of the receivable declines; if EUR appreciates, we benefit but cannot count on that gain. Our primary objective is to stabilize cash flows and protect budgeted revenue. Secondary objectives are to quantify tradeoffs between hedge cost and certainty, and to establish a repeatable process for future exposures.

---

## Methods
We will compare three hedge families:
- **Forward:** lock in a fixed EUR/USD rate; pro: full certainty; con: no upside.
- **Option:** right but not obligation to sell EUR at a strike; pro: downside protection plus upside; con: premium cost.
- **Natural hedge:** match EUR inflows with EUR expenses/debt; pro: low explicit cost; con: limited availability and imperfect offset.

Stage 2 builds an Excel model for scenario analysis and compares unhedged vs. each hedge. Stage 3 documents model logic and designs an improved version. Stage 4 selects a recommendation and prepares the CFO brief.
